Disability Income Assistance Portal — Boulders, Rocks & Pebbles

A user-perspective sizing of the portal scope. Boulders are transformational shifts the portal isn't credible without. Rocks are significant features that each unlock a distinct recipient situation. Pebbles are refinements and polish — quick to ship, additive value.

Boulders — fundamental Rocks — significant features Pebbles — refinements Constraints — apply throughout

Boulders

5

Fundamental to the portal being credible. Without these, it doesn't serve its purpose.

  1. A real-time view of where my file standsStages 3, 4, 10
    File status timeline, action-needed surfacing, behind-the-scenes visibility, decision rendering. Direct read on the brief's "transparency" goal.
  2. The whole payment surfaceStage 5
    Next payment, history with downloadable statements, breakdown, why-it-changed explainers, holds and deductions visibility. What most recipients want most often.
  3. Reporting changes in one placeStage 6
    Single reporting channel with cadence visibility and proactive reminders. Directly attacks the surprise-overpayment harm pattern surfaced in the discourse review.
  4. Third-party access via a delegated-authority shared serviceCross-cutting
    First-class support for trustees, financial administrators, attorneys under POA, family, advocates. Without this, a meaningful share of recipients are locked out at launch. Architectural commitment beyond the portal.
  5. Decision explainability throughoutCross-cutting
    Every payment change, every denial, every approval shown with the rule applied and a link to the policy reference. Direct read on the brief's "accountability" goal.

Rocks

9

Significant features that broaden value. Each one unlocks a distinct recipient situation.

  1. Notifications + preferencesCross-cutting
    SMS and email infrastructure plus the preferences UX (add and verify SMS, choose channels per type, opt out of non-essentials). The infrastructure is foundation; the preferences are the feature on top.
  2. Federal-benefit awarenessCross-cutting
    CDB / DTC / CPP-D context, the dual-$200 distinction (federal CDB vs provincial transition benefit), and a recipient-side confirmation loop. Would have prevented the documented September 2025 CDB-deadline panic.
  3. Document upload back to the workerStage 4 + Stage 6
    General upload channel — covers pre-meeting prep, reassessment evidence, any worker request. Replaces multiple narrower workflows with one consistent surface.
  4. Secure messagingCross-cutting
    Ask a question about my file without phoning. The single feature that most reduces contact-centre load in peer portals.
  5. Appeals surfaceStage 10
    How to challenge a decision, file Notice of Appeal within the 30-day window (with countdown), submit evidence during admin review, case status. The "lock the evidence" warning is the highest-consequence non-obvious rule in the program.
  6. Re-application during the transition windowStage 9 · ephemeral
    Options + tracking for the AISH↔ADAP cohort through Dec 31, 2027. The whole stage is time-bounded but high-value while the window is open.
  7. Personal benefits sub-lifecycleStage 7 · phase 2
    Apply, status, history, browse what's available, see details on a specific benefit. Entitlement uptake is the underlying problem — recipients don't know what they could request.
  8. Overpayments + underpayments handlingStage 8 · phase 2
    Overpayment notice with structured breakdown, exemption request, voluntary repayment warning (critical — waives appeal rights), underpayment notice, repayment agreement self-service.
  9. Persistent recordCross-cutting infrastructure
    Everything submitted, every decision, every payment visible to the recipient (and authorized third parties) for the life of the file. Today recipients keep paper because the system won't tell them what it knows.

Pebbles

12

Refinements and polish. On their own each is a known want; collectively they make the portal feel finished rather than functional.

  1. Annual tax slip downloadStage 5
    Known annual need. Today arrives by mail; the portal adds a downloadable digital copy.
  2. Replacement health benefits card requestStage 5
    Trivial transaction that today consumes phone time.
  3. Proof-of-enrollment letterStage 5
    Distinct from the remittance statement — for third parties (some courts, banks) that require a formal one-page enrollment confirmation.
  4. Personalized greeting + program identifierHome dashboard
    "You're on AISH" at a glance so the recipient knows their track without digging.
  5. Recent activity feedHome dashboard
    Recent events — payments, reports submitted, account changes, decisions. Quick orientation.
  6. Quick actions panelHome dashboard
    Shortcuts to the most common tasks (report a change, upload a document, message my worker).
  7. Reporting history viewStage 6
    Recipient-side sanity check on what they've reported recently. Program-integrity caveat: dates and types by default, dollar impact on click.
  8. File review schedule visibilityStage 6
    Under ADAP financial reviews are annual; medical reviews vary per case. Lets recipients prepare. Needs deeper validation of user demand.
  9. Advocate / Ombudsman connection linksStage 10
    Minimal content surface to the Alberta Advocate for Persons with Disabilities and the Alberta Ombudsman from inside the appeals flow.
  10. Estate authorization + funeral benefits applicatorStage 11 · phase 2
    End-of-life path: authorize an estate representative and guide them through the funeral benefits applicator (multiple manual-mandated caps, 6-month reimbursement window). Lower frequency, high recipient/family stakes when it happens.
  11. Out-of-province exit guidanceStage 11 · phase 2
    Specific residency rules and outside-Alberta absence allowances. Defined exit with its own mechanics.
  12. Handoff to next supportStage 11 · phase 2
    Aging into OAS, leaving for employment, moving to PDD-only support. The portal names the obvious next step and links out; the supporting program owns the actual transition.

Constraints — not boulders / rocks / pebbles

8

These shape how everything above is built, not what gets built. They apply throughout.

  • Accessibility — WCAG 2.2 AA, screen reader, plain language (~grade 6), keyboard nav, generous touch targets, predictable behaviour.
  • Mobile-first — every flow works on a phone over slow data.
  • Tone & dignity — language that treats the recipient as the principal, not a suspect to be verified.
  • Offline / assisted parity — anything possible in the portal is possible by phone or in-person with the same quality.
  • Trust signals — plain language, explicit consent, predictable navigation, no dark patterns, clear pathway out.
  • Error tolerance for high-cost-of-error users — reliable confirmations, reversible actions, critical actions confirmed by a second channel.
  • Multilingual support — beyond English; professionally translated for the most-used languages in the recipient population.
  • Augmentation, not replacement — letters, phone, and in-person channels remain available throughout. The portal sits alongside them, not in place of them.

On using this

  • Boulders are sequencing-critical. They're hard to phase in incrementally without leaving the portal feeling incomplete. Best built together as a foundation wave.
  • Rocks can ship independently. Each one can land on its own and add real value without prerequisites (beyond the boulders + infrastructure).
  • Pebbles are optimization plays. Schedule them when there's slack capacity, or batch a few together for a polish release.
  • Constraints don't sequence. They're applied to whatever is being built, by whoever is building it.
